Agriculture exploration. Mainly italy.

Where are costs? As an agri-tech company, i imagine our focus would be on reducing costs and improving efficiencies.
As someone totally new to agriculture, here is what I identified as the main costs. Targetted for Italy.

Land: rent, or the opportunity cost of owning. Often the single largest line, and especially heavy in Italy where land is expensive and fragmented into small parcels.
Labor: the dividing line between the two worlds in agri: represents tiny cost for mechanized cereals; while its the dominant cost for specialty crops, which need hand-pruning, hand-thinning, hand-harvest. And labor in Italy is scarce, aging, and increasingly expensive (heavy reliance on seasonal migrant workers). This is why orchard/vineyard robotics is one of the hottest areas in ag-tech.
Inputs: fertilizer (nitrogen above all, energy-intensive to make, and prices spiked hard after the 2022 gas crisis); crop protection (fungicides especially, vines get many fungicide passes per season against mildew, a major vineyard cost); seed/planting material; water.
Machinery + energy: capital, depreciation, diesel/gas, plus energy for grain drying, irrigation pumping, greenhouse heating. On small farms machines sit idle, which is why Italy leans heavily on contractors (contoterzisti) who own the equipment.
What Italy actually grows (by value)
Vegetables/horticulture are the largest single sector.
 Wine: the crown jewel. Italy is the world's largest wine producer (~44-48 million hl), on ~720,000 hectares, with €8.1 billion in exports in 2024 (2nd by value, 1st by volume). High value per hectare, geometrically rich (CV and 3D problems), and the sector itself already uses the tech: drones, sensors and vision systems as climate-adaptation tools. The field is badly fragmented: 35% of Italian wine-growers farm under 5 hectares, versus 7% in France, and ~23% of vineyards are already certified organic, which makes fungicide-reduction (spray only where disease risk is real) a concrete selling point. Vegetables, fruit, olive oil. high value, perishable, labor-heavy, disease-exposed (apples in South Tyrol, processing tomatoes in Puglia/Emilia, citrus in the South, olives in Puglia hit hard by both drought and Xylella).
Durum wheat: the squeezed commodity. This is the hard case. Area has fallen below ~1.2M hectares; Italy now imports around 90% of its durum needs, with ~200,000 farms at risk and prices on the Foggia exchange falling sharply (~€310–380/tonne in 2024) under pressure from cheap Russian/Turkish/Canadian imports. The one economic lever is quality: protein content and vitreousness command a premium for pasta-grade grain but the margins are thin and the trend is downward. Lupa FoodsEuropean Parliament

All in all, this short initial exploration of agriculture led me to believe, given our skillset, costs of entry, high heterogeneity of problems across different types of crops and general adversity to large structural changes that characterizes the field, pushing for a yield estimation software as a starting point, which could then expand into other branches and applications, would be a good idea.
The product could focus on:
Capture and localization. Glasshouses have pipe rail (the heating pipes double as picking-trolley rails): a free platform. Italian plastic tunnels in Sicily/Campania often don't, which is precisely why Ecoation built a no-pipe-rail version. In a GNSS-denied greenhouse you need visual-inertial odometry to tag each observation to a row and post, and SLAM in structured rows is the background, not a thing you have to learn.
The reconstruction layer is your moat, because the incumbents count in 2D. The fundamental hard problem in fruit counting is occlusion: greenhouses restrict camera placement, and fruit color/brightness varies across plants, over time, and between camera positions, so fruit hidden behind leaves and other fruit get systematically undercounted, and the same fruit gets double-counted across frames as the camera moves. Multi-view 3D reconstruction / Gaussian splatting or NeRFs attacks all three: you see around occlusions from multiple angles, you track each fruit in 3D so you don't double-count it, and you recover true fruit size and volume. That matters because operators need kilograms, not counts :) and weight is the documented weak link: one recent greenhouse system hit 94.4% accuracy on fruit counting but only 87.5% on weight estimation. 3D sizing → a per-cultivar size-to-weight model → kg is exactly where a 2D counter is weakest and you're strongest. niharxiv
Ripeness staging is the second edge. Incumbents classify maturity (green → breaker → ripe) by color, which drifts with light. Inverse-rendering work recovers illumination-invariant reflectance, BRDFs etc, so the maturity stage is stable across glazing, supplemental LEDs, shade screens, and time of day or other lighting conditions. Ripeness stage is what converts a fruit map into "harvestable in N days." This edge is much larger in Italian plastic tunnels, where light is messier than in a controlled Dutch glasshouse.
Estimating the unseen. Even with 3D you only image part of the canopy, so you still need a sampling-to-total model, a learned occlusion-correction factor per cultivar and training system.

Forecasting layer (tomato specific example)
The perception layer outputs, per zone, a census of fruit by ripeness stage with sizes, i.e., the pipeline of fruit at each maturity. Turning that into a harvest curve leans on the fact that tomato/pepper ripening is strongly temperature-driven: harvest date can be predicted from the date of anthesis plus integrated greenhouse temperature, to within about ±2 days. So the model = current fruit-stage census + climate (temperature, daily light integral) -> when each cohort ripens and what it'll weigh -> kg by week and by zone, short-horizon for labor and medium-horizon for sales, with uncertainty bands.
The catch is calibration: you need the operator's actual harvest weights per zone/day as labels, which means a cold-start per greenhouse and a data flywheel, and it's why Source.ag's cross-greenhouse data is a moat you should not try to out-collect from zero. Compete on perception accuracy (algorithmic, less data-hungry), not on out-forecasting a platform with years of multi-site data.

Tomatoes example study-case
On an indeterminate high-wire tomato plant, fruit ripens continuously. At any single moment the same plant carries flowers, hard green fruit, "breakers" just turning color, and fully ripe fruit all at once, stacked up the stem. What's pickable this week was biologically set weeks ago; what's pickable next week is already hanging on the plant but isn't ready. So every commitment the operator makes, to a buyer or to a labor crew, is a bet on a ripening pipeline they can't yet harvest.
Predicting that pipeline is the operation, and they're squeezed from two directions at once.
The commercial side (selling forward)
Serious operators don't live hand-to-mouth on the daily spot market. They supply supermarket chains through marketers, packers, or cooperatives on programmed supply, agreements to deliver, say, X tons a week of 250g datterino punnets, committed weeks or a whole season ahead. Retailers demand continuous, predictable volume to keep shelves full and to plan promotions, and the Sicilian growers we discussed export precisely this way to German, Austrian, and Polish retail. The marketer who aggregates growers and sells upward needs each grower's forecast to commit those volumes and to negotiate price, because guaranteed, stable supply earns a premium over spot, so a credible forecast literally buys better terms. Hortidaily
The downside is asymmetric and brutal because as the product is perishable:
Under-deliver: you commit 100 tons and ripen 80. Now the marketer either buys the 20-ton gap on the spot market at a worse price (eating the margin) or shorts the supermarket. Shorting a retailer is the cardinal sin: fill-rate penalties, getting dropped from the week's promotion, or losing the listing outright. And a lost listing is the relationship and the bulk of your volume gone, handed to a competitor.
Over-produce: you ripen 120 tons into 100 tons of demand. The surplus 20 tons has to move in days or it rots. You dump it on the wholesale market at fire-sale prices (often below cost) or send it to processing for pennies. You can't warehouse your way out: a ripe tomato has days, not weeks. A grain farmer stores the crop and waits for a better price; you physically cannot.

The labor side (staffing the harvest)
Harvest is the single biggest variable cost, and it's almost entirely manual. Delicate cherry and datterino fruit bruise, so it's hand-picked. Crews (often seasonal labor in Sicily) are booked days ahead: you tell your team or a labor contractor how many pickers you need on which mornings. You can't conjure twenty extra pickers tomorrow if a big flush suddenly comes ripe, and you can't send a booked crew home without paying them (or losing them to the farm down the road).
Picking is time-critical in both directions: too early and the fruit's unripe and downgraded; too late and it's over-ripe, soft, unsellable, or spoiling on the vine. So you have to match picker-hours to each day's ripening load:
Under-staff (you guessed the flush too low): ripe fruit doesn't get picked in time, over-ripens on the plant, and is lost or downgraded. And you can't catch up, because tomorrow has its own load.
Over-staff (you guessed too high): you're paying idle crews standing in the rows with nothing ripe to pick, or pushing them to strip marginal under-ripe fruit just to justify the wage, which wrecks quality.
And that same yield number sizes everything downstream too, packing-line shifts, graders, cold-chain, and transport all have to match the incoming volume. One forecast drives pickers, packhouse, and logistics together.
Why it's hard, and why perception fits
The reason gut-feel fails is that you're estimating a moving, partly hidden target. Much of the fruit is occluded in the canopy; it's at mixed maturity; and the ripening speed rides on temperature and light (roughly degree-days), which shift week to week, so even a perfect count of today's green fruit doesn't tell you when it ripens. On top of that it varies across the house (planting blocks, vigor zones, edges), so any single sample is unrepresentative. The status quo is an agronomist walking the rows, counting fruit on a handful of "representative" plants, multiplying up to the whole greenhouse, and correcting by intuition: a few plants out of tens of thousands, subjective, weekly at best, inconsistent between people. The incumbents sell directly against exactly this.
That's also why the horizon is one to three weeks: it's the length of the ripening pipeline, and it matches the commercial and labor planning windows. Inside that window the fruit physically exists on the plant but isn't yet pickable, predictable from observation plus a temperature model. Shorter than that, you can already just see what's ripe; longer, and the fruit isn't even set yet, which is a harder, different problem.
And here's why a perception system is the right hammer: the binding constraint is measurement: nobody can see and count the future load accurately. A camera moving the rows censuses effectively every plant instead of ten, catching the spatial variation sampling misses; it stages each fruit by maturity, so you get the timing pipeline rather than just a total; and it does so consistently and georeferenced, so you can stage labor zone by zone. Fold in the temperature model and "fruit census now" becomes "kilograms harvestable per day, per zone": the exact number both the marketer and the crew scheduler are missing. Measuring fruit at scale, under occlusion, is a computer-vision and 3D problem
Why they'd pay: the cost of being wrong recurs every single week -> penalties, lost listings, dumped fruit, idle crews, rot, so even a few points of forecast accuracy compound into real money on a high-value perishable at scale. The fact that Source.ag made news simply for improving forecast accuracy 33% at the three-week horizon tells you accuracy is the competitive battleground, and that operators treat it as worth paying for. Let’s see a breakdown of the costs for tomato and apple crops:
Greenhouse tomatoes (Sicily). A 30-farm Sicilian survey of cherry tomatoes found total production cost around 20.87 €/m², of which ~78% is variable cost, with labor the single largest item at 41.6% of total cost, materials and services at 35.5%, and interest ~1%. So the rough hierarchy is
Labor (~40–45%, the dominant cost): hand transplanting, training/de-leafing, harvest. This is the line item, and it's why your forecasting-for-labor pitch lands on the right nerve.
Materials & inputs (~35%): grafted seedlings (expensive), substrate (for soil-less), the fertigation nutrient solution, biocontrol/crop protection, water, and packaging.
Structure + energy + interest (the rest): greenhouse amortization, plastic film (replaced every few years, plus storm damage), and notably low energy compared to the Dutch: Sicilian plastic tunnels are mostly unheated, whereas Dutch greenhouses' energy costs pushed wholesale cherry tomato prices to €3.50/kg. The Sicilian farm-gate price is around €1.2/kg, and margins are thin and price-volatile.
NB, info to verify -> Production is fragmented across small-to-medium family operations averaging ~2 ha (consolidating, with the bigger ones surviving): each farm owns or rents its land, owns its greenhouse structures, runs the growing, and hires (often seasonal/migrant) labor. Marketing is then handled by producer organizations, cooperatives, or marketers who aggregate many growers, grade, pack, and sell: taking a commission. Larger operators (like Licitra at ~100 ha) integrate more of the chain themselves, but the norm is fragmented production plus aggregated marketing. True "owns-everything-from-land-to-retail" vertical integration exists mainly among the biggest players and is the exception, not the rule. 




What crop should we focus on then? We established that it is something grown in greenhouses. Which one though?
Ok lets say we want to start validating something, what we are looking for is the veggie/fruit with the shortest predict-then-check loops we can get.
We want to have a very short
capture -> forecast -> wait for the actual harvest -> compare -> improve. 
So, which crop gives us the most prediction-vs-harvest data points in the least time? 
Apples, grapes, wheat, and single-cut field vegetables are the worst possible choices for our startup. One validation per year, we fail once, we die :(
What we want instead is a controlled-environment, continuously-harvested, fruit-dense crop. 
We basically have 2 options to start:
Tabletop everbearing/day-neutral strawberry: this is the fastest validator, and should be the main pick if iteration speed is the only criterion. Flower-to-ripe is only ~3-4 weeks and it's picked every 2-3 days, and day-neutral varieties (varieties that produce fruit continuously from late spring through early fall, unaffected by day length) fruit continuously for months. Our forecast horizon is short (days to ~two weeks), so we validate a one-week forecast in one week, over and over. Three bonuses: tabletop systems hang the fruit below the canopy, so occlusion, the thing that slows a fruit-counting model's convergences, is minimal, which is a good thing for getting the perception engine accurate fast; it's high-value, perishable, and brutally labor-intensive to pick, so a forecast is genuinely wanted; and there's precedent -> the Sensonomic pilot we found earlier was exactly strawberry-under-tunnel. Caveats: the berries are small (detection is finicky, though tabletop mitigates it) and extremely perishable.
Greenhouse indeterminate tomato: our close second, and tbh the more market-proven option. A long indeterminate cycle runs 8-11 months and is harvested weekly, so we get ~30-40 validation events from a single crop, and it's hugely data-rich (hundreds of fruit per plant). It's the market we’ve already sized, and it's where the incumbents validate. The tradeoffs versus strawberry: a slightly longer forecast horizon (1-3 weeks) and more canopy occlusion, so each loop is marginally slower, but the market is much bigger.

What does the product provide?
Existing approaches rely on 2D detection and segmentation. What does 3D reconstruction give us? Volume 🙂
Weekly or daily camera pass enables a 3d digital twin of the tomato plantation. What we basically sell to the customer is “KGs harvestable per week, per zone”.
As prev said, we can estimate actual fruit volume, so we can estimate weight via some cultivar-specific model. Operators schedule labor and commit in KGs, not count.
Occlusion-robust, de-duplication counts. Multi-view reconstruction sees partially hidden fruit from different angles, we have 3d representation. Often 2d counting ends up double counting, which skews results.
Illumination-invariant ripeness. I have ideas of how to de-light reconstructed scenes. This would allow for more precise ripeness estimation / input to whatever ripeness model we are using.

With weekly passes we can enable a 4D digital twin of the plants, tracking fruit growth trajectories, surface anomalies, sickness predictions etc. That’s the pandora vase for later, and which I think can become what we should call “Earth Model” in the vein of “World Model” to excite investors :)

MVP_v1 - prove value to a customer (one cycle, 1-3 design partners).
Capture: concierge. You walk a handheld or cart-mounted RGB camera down a set of fixed reference rows weekly (a moving camera naturally gives the multi-view overlap 3DGS needs, a 360 camera is even better (insta360 or DJI osmo 360)), localized with SLAM so the same zones line up week to weekt.
Scope cut hard: We should not start reconstructing the whole damn greenhouse, lets scope it to 1 or 2 serras. No disease/scouting/climate. Keep the forecast crude, a thermal-time ripening curve calibrated on the partner's data is enough; the perception is the moat, not the forecasting model.
Output: a weekly report — kg by ripeness stage by zone, plus a 1–3 week harvest forecast for those zones.
Validate: compare our predicted kg-by-week-by-zone against the partner's actual harvest logs. Indeterminate tomato is harvested weekly for months, we can accumulate dozens of prediction-vs-actual events in a single cycle, so we can show statistically meaningful accuracy in one season.
Success = two numbers: you beat the partner's current method (manual sampling/gut feeling), and you beat a 2D baseline on weight specifically — because that's the claim that makes you better than the incumbents, not just better than guessing.
